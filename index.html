teaching

I have taught English as a second language in Romania, China, Peru, and France. Currently I am working on a project called Yes, And...:Improvisational Games for Teachers. I would welcome help from any other coders who would like to work on the webpage!

In 2003, I finished a Master's degree from the University of Chicago, and in 2010 defended a PhD thesis on 19th century U.S. literature and culture at the University of Iowa. As a graduate student, I taught a variety of courses, including introduction to literature, comic and tragic literature, and American lives.

At Grandview University in Des Moines, Iowa, I worked as an adjunct professor, using classroom technology such as smart boards and class wikis, as well as open source texts and educational materials.

writing

My self-published speculative fantasy novel, Runeshadow, the Bard of Broken Spells, is available for free download in the following formats:

  • epub
  • pdf
  • txt
  • mobi

My graduate thesis, Evolutionary Landscapes: Adaptation, Selection, and Mutation in 19th Century Literary Ecologies uses evolutionary theory to analyze lesser known works of Edgar Allan Poe, Herman Melville, and obscure utopian authors, online . My interest in the "ecologies" in which various kinds of texts are selectively replicated led directly into my interest in open source software and creative commons licensing.

I also maintain a blog, Ecological Humanist, which is a incongrous mix of science fiction book reviews, personal essays, and random observations.

coding

I am currently learning front end web development, including html5, css, and javascript. I've been working with bootstrap, jquery, and am beginning to learn how to implement Angular.js

While living abroad, I use dthe internet to offer remote copy editing and online tutoring, and became more interested in the ways in which educational resources and information was distributed, licensed, and produced. As with other languages I use, I felt the need to understand more of the underlying logic and grammar that encodes the way information is shared on the internet. In the spirit of "learning by doing," I began to improve my coding skills by creating digital projects, both creative and academic.

My interest in coding, however, goes back at least until 2003 in Chicago, when I wrote this entry for the University of Chicago's Theories of Media keywords glossary. Kembrew McLeod, at the University of Iowa, was instrumental in fostering my appreciation of and commitment to opensource and copyleft philosophies. I believe in the value of a creative and decentralized internet, where the tools for creating responsive, sophisticated content are as accessible and distributed as possible. My ultimate goal is to create open source educational resources and applications, and to contribute to creative online communities and non-profits.
